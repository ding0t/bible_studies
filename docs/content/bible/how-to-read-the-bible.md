# How to read the Bible

The single most useful idea I know for reading Scripture well comes from Gordon Fee and Douglas Stuart's *How to Read the Bible for All Its Worth*: every passage has two separate questions attached to it, and they have to be asked in order.

1. **Exegesis** — what did this mean to the people who first read or heard it?
2. **Hermeneutics** — what does it mean for us, now?

Almost every bad reading of the Bible I've come across skipped straight to question 2. The fix isn't complicated, just disciplined: don't answer "what does this mean for me" until you've genuinely answered "what did this mean to them." A verse ripped straight out of its own moment and applied directly to yours will usually mean something the author never intended — sometimes harmlessly, sometimes not.

## Ask these questions, in order

**1. What kind of writing is this?**
A psalm, a letter, a law, a parable, and a vision are read differently, on purpose — see [Reading by genre](#reading-by-genre) below. Knowing the genre before you start tells you what kind of questions are even fair to ask of the text.

**2. Who wrote it, to whom, and why?**
The historical occasion — author, audience, date, and the actual circumstance that called the passage into being. A letter answering a specific controversy in a specific church reads differently once you know what the controversy was.

**3. What comes immediately before and after?**
Don't exegete a verse in isolation from its paragraph. Ask what the author's own train of thought is — what's the point of this unit, in the author's own argument, not a point you already had in mind before you opened the book.

**4. What do the words actually say?**
Most of the time the words are plain enough in translation. When a specific word is doing real theological work, or two good translations render it noticeably differently, it's worth checking the underlying Hebrew or Greek rather than guessing — see [Hebrew Studies](../hebrew-studies/index.md) for a start on the original languages this site leans on.

**5. What's tied to that moment, and what carries over?**
Some things in a passage are bound to its original cultural setting; some things transcend it. This is the actual hinge between exegesis and hermeneutics — get honest about which is which before you draw a principle out of the text.

**6. Does this hold up elsewhere in Scripture?**
Don't build a doctrine on one obscure narrative detail or an incidental remark. A narrative usually *illustrates* a principle taught plainly somewhere else; it rarely *establishes* one by itself. If you can't find the principle stated directly anywhere else, hold the conclusion loosely.

## Reading by genre

Different kinds of biblical writing were written to work differently, and reading all of them the same way is where a lot of bad interpretation starts.

| Genre | Read for |
| --- | --- |
| **Epistles** (letters) | What problem in the original church occasioned this passage? Does the author himself mark a claim as universal ("in all the churches"), or as local practice? |
| **Old Testament narrative** | Narrative *records* what happened — it doesn't automatically endorse it. Does the narrator, or the rest of Scripture, evaluate this as good or bad? Or is that left for you to notice? |
| **Acts / historical precedent** | Was the story's own point to establish a repeatable pattern, or is the detail incidental to something else the author's actually making a point about? A pattern isn't automatically a command. |
| **Gospels** | Which Gospel, and what's that author's own emphasis and audience? Read each Gospel on its own terms before harmonizing it with the others. |
| **Parables** | What's the one main point the original hearers would have grasped? Who is Jesus needling, and what situation provoked the story? Resist reading meaning into every incidental detail. |
| **Law** | Moral, civil, or ceremonial? Even where a specific rule doesn't carry over today, what does it reveal about God's own character? |
| **Prophets** | Read first as God's people being held to an existing covenant — blessing for faithfulness, judgment for breach — in the prophet's own time, before reading it forward to any other time. |
| **Psalms** | What kind of psalm — lament, praise, thanksgiving, a royal psalm, an imprecatory one? A psalm is usually a prayer addressed to God, not a proposition addressed to you. Let its form shape what you expect from it. |
| **Wisdom literature** | Proverbs are generally-true observations, not unconditional promises. Hold that hopeful realism against Job and Ecclesiastes' more honest counterpoint, rather than flattening one against the other. |
| **Revelation / apocalyptic** | Find the Old Testament imagery a vision is drawing on before assigning it a novel, modern meaning. Remember it was written to seven real churches first — that pastoral purpose sits right alongside the future-oriented vision, not instead of it. |

## A few habits worth keeping

- **Compare translations.** Where two solid translations render a verse noticeably differently, that's worth noticing, not smoothing over.
- **Use commentaries last, not first.** Read the text yourself before you read what someone else concluded about it — a commentary should check your reading, not form it for you.
- **Name your sources.** If a conclusion came from a commentary, a lexicon, or someone else's teaching, say so. It's not a weakness to cite where an idea came from; it's how a reader can go verify it themselves.
