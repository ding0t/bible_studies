# Israel and the Church

Is the Church a separate program from Israel, or is the Church actually Israel itself, under a
different name at a later stage of the same story? Hebrew Roots teacher Eddie Chumney argues for
the latter — that "church" and "Israel" are, biblically, the same body.

## The case for "the Church is Israel"

Summarizing Chumney's argument: Israel became "a holy nation" at Sinai ([Exodus 19:5-6
(ESV)](https://www.blueletterbible.org/esv/Exo/19/5-6)), entering a betrothal relationship with God
([Jeremiah 2:2-3 (ESV)](https://www.blueletterbible.org/esv/Jer/2/2-3)) as a "mixed multitude" of
Israelite and non-Israelite alike who had come out of Egypt together
([Exodus 12:38 (ESV)](https://www.blueletterbible.org/esv/Exo/12/38)) — on this
reading, that assembly (*kahal*/*ekklesia*) at Sinai *is* the church in its first form. After
Solomon's reign the nation split into the northern kingdom (Israel) and southern kingdom (Judah);
the northern kingdom was cut off from that covenant standing
([Hosea 1 (ESV)](https://www.blueletterbible.org/esv/Hos/1/1)). Gentile believers are later
grafted into that same olive root through Christ ([Romans 11:17-24
(ESV)](https://www.blueletterbible.org/esv/Rom/11/17-24)), having formerly been "strangers to the
covenants of promise" ([Ephesians 2:11-12
(ESV)](https://www.blueletterbible.org/esv/Eph/2/11-12)). Ezekiel's promise that the two
kingdoms — pictured as two sticks — will one day become one again in God's hand ([Ezekiel 37:15-27
(ESV)](https://www.blueletterbible.org/esv/Eze/37/15-27)) points, on this reading, to a future
reunification of a single people that redemptive history has only ever divided, never actually
multiplied.

Chumney states the conclusion directly:

> "The nation of Israel were a church / Kahal / Ekklesia at Mount Sinai... So, Biblically, the
> terms 'church' and 'Israel' are synonymous terms."

## Cautions

This site holds a dispensational view of prophecy (see [Statement of
Faith](../../about/statement-of-faith.md#end-times)), which classically treats Israel and the
Church as distinct programs with distinct promises rather than one interchangeable body — reading
the Romans 11 olive tree, for instance, as a single root supporting two distinguishable kinds of
branches rather than one undifferentiated tree, and reserving Israel's specific, unconditional land
and kingdom promises to Israel rather than treating them as transferred to the Church. The "Israel
is the Church" reading above sits in real tension with that: if the two labels are simply
synonyms across redemptive history, there's no clear ground left for keeping their promises
distinct either. That tension is worth sitting with — Ephesians 2, Romans 11, and Ezekiel 37 all
have to be read together, not selectively — rather than resolved by picking a label.
