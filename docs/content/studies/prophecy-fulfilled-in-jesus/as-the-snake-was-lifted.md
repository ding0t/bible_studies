# As the Snake Was Lifted Up: The Bronze Serpent and the Son of Man

## The two passages

> ✝️ Numbers 21:4-9 (ESV)
>
> 4 From Mount Hor they set out by the way to the Red Sea, to go around the land of Edom. And the people became impatient on the way. 5 And the people spoke against God and against Moses, "Why have you brought us up out of Egypt to die in the wilderness? For there is no food and no water, and we loathe this worthless food." 6 Then the LORD sent fiery serpents among the people, and they bit the people, so that many people of Israel died. 7 And the people came to Moses and said, "We have sinned, for we have spoken against the LORD and against you. Pray to the LORD, that he take away the serpents from us." So Moses prayed for the people. 8 And the LORD said to Moses, "Make a fiery serpent and set it on a pole, and everyone who is bitten, when he sees it, shall live." 9 So Moses made a bronze serpent and set it on a pole. And if a serpent bit anyone, he would look at the bronze serpent and live.

> ✝️ John 3:14-16 (ESV)
>
> 14 And as Moses lifted up the serpent in the wilderness, so must the Son of Man be lifted up, 15 that whoever believes in him may have eternal life. 16 For God so loved the world, that he gave his only Son, that whoever believes in him should not perish but have eternal life.

Jesus himself draws this connection directly -- this isn't a typology later interpreters invented and read back into the text. That matters for how the study proceeds: the connection between the two passages is textually secure from the start; the exegetical work is understanding each passage on its own terms first, then seeing precisely how Jesus applies the first to himself.

## Historical and cultural context

**Numbers' setting.** Numbers 21:4-9 falls late in Israel's wilderness years, shortly after Aaron's death (Numbers 20:22-29) and Israel being refused passage through Edom, forcing a difficult detour "by the way to the Red Sea" (21:4). This is very likely the last of the wilderness generation's great "murmuring" episodes -- the same complaint-judgment-confession-mercy pattern that runs through Exodus 16, Numbers 11, Numbers 14, and Numbers 16 -- and it sits right at the narrative hinge: the itinerary notices that follow (21:10-20) and the victories over Sihon and Og (21:21-35) immediately turn the story from wandering toward conquest. Even the last rebellion of the generation about to inherit the promise is met with both real judgment and a real, freely-provided remedy.

**The complaint and the judgment.** The people's specific complaint -- "there is no food and no water, and we loathe this worthless food" (21:5) -- is aimed squarely at God's own provision (the manna). "The LORD sent fiery serpents among the people" (21:6) is covenant enforcement, not arbitrary cruelty: it answers rebellion against God's provision with an immediate, visible consequence, matching the same shape of judgment-and-mercy found throughout Israel's wilderness history.

**Serpent imagery in the ancient world.** Serpent symbolism was religiously loaded across the ancient Near East -- Egypt's royal cobra emblem (the uraeus), worn on Pharaoh's crown, associated with protection and royal authority, is the best-known example, and Israel had just come out of Egypt. Against that backdrop, God's instruction to Moses is a pointed inversion of the expected use of serpent imagery: make an image of the very creature that is killing the people, and be healed simply by looking at it in obedient trust -- not by treating the object as an amulet with power of its own.

**The object's later history proves the point.** Centuries later, this same bronze serpent -- by then given the name Nehushtan (2 Kings 18:4) -- had become an idol that Israel was burning incense to. King Hezekiah destroyed it as part of his religious reforms, alongside the high places, the sacred pillars, and the Asherah pole. That's explicit textual evidence that the object itself never carried any inherent power; venerating the artifact instead of trusting the God who commanded its use was condemned as idolatry, not preserved as a relic worth keeping.

## Literary context

**Numbers 21:4-9** functions as the last "murmuring and judgment" unit of the wilderness period, positioned exactly where the book's narrative turns from wandering toward conquest. Its governing point: obedient, simple trust in God's provided remedy -- not further merit or ritual -- is what turns judgment into life, even for a generation whose entire history up to this point has been marked by unbelief.

**John 3:14-15** sits inside Jesus's night conversation with Nicodemus (John 3:1-21), directly after Jesus rebukes him -- "the teacher of Israel" -- for not understanding the new-birth teaching "from these things," implying Nicodemus should already have grasped it from the Hebrew Scriptures he knew (3:10). Jesus then reaches for exactly this text: "as Moses lifted up the serpent in the wilderness, so must the Son of Man be lifted up" (3:14). What follows matters for how the passage is usually read: checked directly against the Greek, John 3:16 ("Οὕτως γὰρ..." "For God so loved...") opens with a connective continuing the very same thought as 3:14-15, not a fresh, free-standing statement -- the identical clause "whoever believes in him... may/should have eternal life" appears in both 3:15 and 3:16. John 3:16 is the immediate continuation and explanation of the serpent typology, not a stand-alone verse to be lifted out on its own.

The same "lifted up" language recurs later in John: "I, when I am lifted up from the earth, will draw all people to myself" (John 12:32) -- and John removes any doubt about what the phrase means by adding his own explanatory note: "he said this to show by what kind of death he was going to die" (12:33). "Lifted up" is John's settled term for the crucifixion specifically, used well before it narratively happens (this conversation with Nicodemus is chapter 3 of 21) -- an early, veiled prediction of the cross built directly on this Old Testament type rather than announced as direct prophecy.

## Word studies

**Nachash / nechoshet (נָחָשׁ / נְחֹשֶׁת, "serpent" / "bronze," H5175 / H5178).** Numbers 21:9's description of what Moses made -- literally *nachash nechoshet*, "a serpent of bronze" -- is a deliberate wordplay in the Hebrew text itself, not an accident of translation. The sound-play underlines the point: what's made is precisely an image of the very thing killing the people, not a different, safer symbol substituted for it.

**Saraph (שָׂרָף, "fiery/burning one," H8314).** This word describes both the venomous serpents themselves ("fiery serpents," Numbers 21:6, 8) and, in an entirely different context, the six-winged celestial beings attending God's throne in Isaiah's temple vision ("the seraphim," Isaiah 6:2, 6) -- the same Hebrew lexeme in both places. The shared root sense is "burning" (most likely describing the burning sensation of the venomous bite, for the wilderness serpents), but this is a shared root, not evidence that the two are the same kind of being -- worth being explicit about, since it would be easy to overclaim an identity the text doesn't actually make.

**Nes (נֵס, "standard, banner, pole," H5251).** The pole the bronze serpent was set on (Numbers 21:8-9) uses the same word Isaiah later uses for the messianic banner the nations will seek out: "the root of Jesse... who shall stand as a signal [*nes*] for the peoples" (Isaiah 11:10). A real verbal link between the object lifted up for Israel's healing and later messianic banner-imagery -- worth noting as a genuine intertextual resonance in the Hebrew vocabulary, without overclaiming Isaiah 11:10 is a conscious, deliberate citation of the Numbers scene.

**ὑψόω (*hypsoō*, "to lift up / exalt," G5312) -- the load-bearing term of this whole study.** This is the verb John uses both times in 3:14 (Moses "lifted up" the serpent; the Son of Man "must be lifted up") and again at 12:32, and John's own gloss (12:33) fixes its meaning as the crucifixion specifically, not a vague or merely figurative exaltation. The word carries real theological weight, though, because of where else it appears: the Greek Old Testament (Septuagint) renders Isaiah's Servant Song with this identical verb -- "Behold, my servant shall deal wisely; he shall be lifted up [*hypsōthēsetai*] and glorified exceedingly" (Isaiah 52:13, LXX, translating a Hebrew original that stacks three separate verbs for exaltation: *yarum* / *nissa* / *gavah*, "he will be high, and lifted up, and greatly exalted"). John's repeated choice of *hypsoō* for Jesus's crucifixion -- rather than the more neutral *stauroō*, "crucify," used elsewhere in the New Testament -- carries forward this same Isaianic double sense: the very act of being "lifted up" in judicial death is simultaneously exaltation and glorification. This isn't reading a modern pun into John that isn't there; the cross as simultaneously humiliation and glory is a recognized Johannine theme independent of this specific word, and the deliberate lexical choice of *hypsoō* is itself evidence the double meaning is intentional, not accidental.

**ὄφις (*ophis*, "serpent," G3789).** The word John uses for the bronze serpent in 3:14 is the identical Greek word Revelation later uses for Satan: "the great dragon... the ancient serpent [*ho ophis ho archaios*], who is called the devil and Satan" (Revelation 12:9). That word choice ties together a chain running through the whole of Scripture: Genesis 3's serpent, placed under a curse, with the promise that the woman's offspring would one day crush its head (Genesis 3:14-15); Numbers 21's bronze image of that same kind of creature, lifted up as the means of healing; John 3:14's direct application of that image to Christ; and Revelation 12:9's naming of the ultimate deceiver as "the ancient serpent" himself. Christ is lifted up bearing the very form associated with the curse and the deceiver, precisely in order to break the curse and defeat the deceiver -- the same shape of argument Paul makes explicitly, in his own vocabulary, elsewhere in the New Testament (see below).

## Theological principle

God's remedy for sin and death takes the form of the very thing under judgment, without itself sharing the fault that brought the judgment, and is received by simple, direct trust rather than any further ritual or merit. The bronze serpent had no venom of its own; it was shaped like the very thing killing Israel, yet anyone who looked at it in obedience lived. Paul states the same principle directly, in his own theological vocabulary, independent of the serpent image: God made Christ, "who knew no sin, to be sin for us" (2 Corinthians 5:21, ESV), and sent his Son "in the likeness of sinful flesh" to "condemn sin in the flesh" (Romans 8:3, ESV) -- Christ bears the likeness of what is under judgment, "yet without sin" (Hebrews 4:15, ESV). Because this same principle is taught explicitly elsewhere in the New Testament, rather than resting on the serpent typology alone, it isn't an isolated inference built on one Old Testament narrative detail.

## Key lessons

- **The remedy God provides often takes the shape of the very problem it heals.** The bronze serpent looked exactly like what was killing Israel; Christ was made "to be sin" and sent "in the likeness of sinful flesh" -- without himself ever sinning. God doesn't rescue by pretending the problem isn't real; he confronts it in its own likeness and defeats it there.
- **The remedy is appropriated by looking, not by working.** "Everyone who is bitten, when he sees it, shall live" (Numbers 21:8) -- no further ritual, payment, or achievement is asked of the dying Israelite beyond looking at what God provided. John's "whoever believes in him" (John 3:15-16) is the exact same shape of response: simple, direct trust in what's been provided, not additional merit.
- **The provision can be corrupted into an idol if it's mistaken for the point.** Israel eventually worshiped the bronze serpent itself (2 Kings 18:4) rather than the God who commanded its use -- a warning that any means of grace, however God-ordained, can be turned into a substitute for trusting God himself.
- **Jesus read his own coming death directly out of the Old Testament, well before it happened.** The "lifted up" language of John 3:14 is a deliberate, pre-planned reading of Numbers 21 applied to the cross -- not a connection made only after the fact by later Christian reflection.
- **Being "lifted up" in shame is, in John's own vocabulary, simultaneously being lifted up in glory.** The same word carries both senses, echoing Isaiah's Servant who is "lifted up and glorified exceedingly" -- the cross is not a detour from Christ's glory but, in John's telling, the very moment of it.

## Discussion questions

1. Does it change how you read John 3:16 to see it as the direct continuation of the bronze-serpent comparison in 3:14-15, rather than a stand-alone verse?
2. The Israelites weren't asked to do anything except look at the bronze serpent. Is there a temptation, in your own faith, to add something to "look and live" / "believe and live" that Scripture doesn't actually require?
3. Israel eventually worshiped the very object God gave them for healing. Are there good things in your own life -- even God-given ones -- at risk of quietly becoming the object of your trust instead of the God who gave them?
4. How does it strike you that Christ is described as being made "in the likeness of sinful flesh" and "to be sin" for us -- taking the very form of what he came to defeat?
