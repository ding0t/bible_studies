## Overview

This is a companion piece to the [existing study on this healing](woman-suffering-bleeding.md), which traces its fulfillment of Malachi 4:2, Isaiah 53:5, and Zechariah 8:23. This study asks a different question: read as a piece of Markan narrative on its own terms, before any prophecy-fulfillment lens is applied, what is this passage actually doing — historically, structurally, and in its own vocabulary?

## Historical and cultural context

Mark's Gospel is traditionally attributed to John Mark, recording the preaching of Peter (a tradition going back to Papias, early second century, preserved in Eusebius). Its audience appears to be Gentile and likely Roman: Mark pauses to translate Aramaic phrases and explain Jewish customs that a Jewish audience wouldn't need explained (e.g. Mark 7:3-4 on handwashing).

The woman's condition — a chronic, non-menstrual discharge of blood lasting twelve years (Mark 5:25) — falls under the purity law of **Leviticus 15:19-30**. The relevant statute:

> "If a woman has a discharge of blood for many days, not at the time of her menstrual impurity, or if she has a discharge beyond the time of her impurity, all the days of the discharge she shall continue in uncleanness, as in the days of her impurity. She shall be unclean." (Leviticus 15:25, ESV)

And crucially, what that uncleanness *does* to others:

> "And whoever touches her bed shall wash his clothes and bathe himself in water and be unclean until the evening... And whoever touches these things shall be unclean." (Leviticus 15:21, 27, ESV)

The Hebrew is direct about the mechanism: the one who **touches** (נָגַע, *naga*, H5060, TWOT #1293) a person or object in this state **becomes unclean** (טָמֵא, *tame*, H2930, TWOT #809) himself. Contact transmits impurity outward from the unclean person to whoever touches them. That is the physics this passage is quietly assuming and then reversing. Coming up to Jesus through a pressing crowd would also have brought her into unavoidable, unnoticed contact with many other people along the way — spreading the same impurity further without any of them knowing it, on the standard reading of Leviticus 15:26-27.

Beyond the ritual dimension, the cost was material: Mark notes she "had suffered much under many physicians, and had spent all that she had" (Mark 5:26) — a detail Luke, traditionally a physician himself (Colossians 4:14), handles more gently in some manuscripts, while Mark states it bluntly. Twelve years of chronic illness in an agrarian economy with no institutional safety net meant financial ruin on top of physical suffering. The uncleanness itself likely compounded the loss: background sources note that Jewish women typically married young, that intercourse was forbidden during this kind of discharge, and that prevailing Pharisaic custom favored divorce where a marriage produced no children — meaning a chronic condition like hers had probably either prevented marriage altogether or ended one. There's also a dark irony worth naming: one common treatment in ancient medicine, alongside genuine herbal knowledge mixed with folk tradition, was bleeding the patient — her twelve years "under the care of many doctors" may have included treatments that worsened the very thing they aimed to cure.

## Literary context: the Markan sandwich

The healing does not stand alone. It sits inside a single narrative unit, **Mark 5:21-43**, built in Mark's characteristic "sandwich" (intercalation) structure:

1. **5:21-24** — Jairus, a synagogue ruler, begs Jesus to come heal his dying twelve-year-old daughter.
2. **5:25-34** — *Interruption*: the woman with the twelve-year discharge is healed on the way.
3. **5:35-43** — The frame resumes: the daughter has died, and Jesus raises her.

Mark uses this technique elsewhere (e.g. the fig tree and temple cleansing in 11:12-25) specifically so the two halves interpret each other. Here, the parallel is pointed: a condition of twelve years' standing (the woman's illness) meets a life of twelve years' standing (the girl's age) — both cases humanly hopeless, both touching the two major categories of ritual defilement in the Torah (chronic discharge, Leviticus 15; corpse contact, Numbers 19:11-13), and both involving people at opposite ends of the social scale: a named synagogue official's daughter, and an unnamed, impoverished, chronically unclean woman. Mark's own interruption makes the point without stating it: Jesus's access is not reserved for those with standing enough to ask him directly.

**The governing question of the unit — "what's the point?"** — is answered by the double resolution: nothing is too socially low or too physically/ritually hopeless to be outside Jesus's reach, and faith, not status, is what he responds to in both halves.

## Word-by-word walkthrough with original-language notes

Working from the Greek text (word data below queried directly from this project's own `bible-text.db`, built from the MACULA Greek dataset — lemma, Strong's number, and Louw-Nida semantic domain per word):

### "Touched" — ἅπτω (*haptō*, G680)

The woman "touched" Jesus's garment (5:27), and asks herself "if I touch" (5:28); Jesus asks "who touched me?" (5:30-31). Concordancing this verb across the rest of Mark turns up something worth noticing: every *other* occurrence of *haptō* in the Gospel (1:41, leper; 3:10; 6:56; 7:33, deaf man; 8:22, blind man; 10:13, children) has **Jesus initiating the touch**, or the sick being brought *to* him to be touched. This passage is the only place in Mark where someone touches Jesus first — and she does it covertly, from behind, in a crowd, precisely because Leviticus 15:27 makes that contact one-directionally defiling under the law: the toucher becomes unclean, not the other way around. The whole scene turns on that expectation being reversed. Power moves outward from Jesus (5:30); nothing moves the other way. Background-commentary work on this passage lands on the same reading independently: *"Despite the expectation that Jesus would be viewed as being rendered unclean, he makes her act known... Jesus was willing to be identified with our uncleanness to make us whole"* (*NIV Cultural Backgrounds Study Bible*, Zondervan, ed. Walton & Keener, at Mark 5:27).

### "Power" — δύναμις (*dynamis*, G1411)

Jesus "perceived in himself that power had gone out from him" (5:30) — the healing is registered as a real transfer, and notably it happens **at her touch**, before he has said or done anything toward her. Most Markan healings up to this point occur at Jesus's spoken word or his own initiating touch; this one happens simply through contact with him, which is itself part of what the narrative is highlighting about who he is.

### "Made well / healed" — σῴζω (*sōzō*, G4982)

This is the word worth slowing down on. The woman says "if I touch his garments, I will be **healed**" (σωθήσομαι, 5:28); Jesus tells her "your faith has **healed** you" (σέσωκέν, 5:34). *Sōzō* is the same verb the New Testament uses for salvation in the fullest theological sense.

Working the word through Mark specifically (word-study-method.md's "author in general" step): concordancing *sōzō* across the whole Gospel shows it spans a real range — purely physical restoration in this passage and at 6:56, but unmistakably soteriological at 8:35 ("whoever loses his life... will save it"), 10:26 ("who then can be saved?"), 13:13, 13:20, and 16:16. The Louw-Nida domain data in our own dataset classifies the 5:28/5:34 occurrences under domain 23.136 (physical healing) — the same domain as *iaomai* ("she was healed," 5:29), Mark's other, unambiguously physical healing verb used two verses earlier. That domain classification is a useful check against over-reading: in context, "healed" is the primary and controlling sense here, not "saved" in the fuller soteriological sense — claiming she "received salvation" in 5:34 would be importing 8:35's usage into a different context, the classic root-fallacy-adjacent error of assuming one word always carries its full semantic range everywhere it appears.

That said, the ambiguity is not simply resolved and dismissed. The exact phrase "**your faith has healed you**" (ἡ πίστις σου σέσωκέν σε) recurs *verbatim* at Mark 10:52, spoken to blind Bartimaeus — not a coincidence of Mark's limited vocabulary, but a deliberate authorial echo linking these two faith-and-healing scenes across the Gospel. Mark appears to be exploiting the word's dual range on purpose: the physical sense governs the immediate scene, while the word's own wider currency in his Gospel keeps the fuller resonance available to an attentive reader, without the text itself making that theological claim explicit at 5:34.

### "Daughter" — θυγάτηρ (*thygatēr*, G2364)

Jesus's address, "Daughter, your faith has healed you" (5:34), is the only place in any Gospel where Jesus calls a woman "daughter" directly. After twelve years of likely exclusion from ordinary family and community touch, he answers her with kinship language — not just healing but restoration into relationship.

## Translation comparison

Mark 5:34's *sesōken* is rendered "made you well" (ESV, WEB) and "made thee whole" (ASV, in the KJV tradition). "Whole" is arguably the better English word here for carrying *sōzō*'s double resonance — physical wholeness and the broader sense of completeness — though "made well" is not wrong, just narrower. Worth noting as a translation choice rather than an error either way.

Matthew's parallel (9:20-22) is markedly shorter than Mark's — roughly a third the length by word count (48 Greek words against Mark's 153 for the equivalent material) — and drops the physicians/expense detail entirely, consistent with Matthew's general habit of compressing Mark's fuller miracle narratives toward the core christological point. Luke's account (8:43-48), per a widely-noted (though here unverified against the textual apparatus) tradition connecting Luke's authorship to his vocation as a physician (Colossians 4:14), is somewhat gentler on the failed physicians than Mark's blunt "spent all that she had... was no better, but rather grew worse" (5:26).

## Theological principle

Faith that reaches for Christ — even hesitantly, covertly, from social and ritual disqualification — meets a power that reverses the expected direction of contamination. Under the law, contact with her should have made *him* unclean. Instead, contact with *him* makes *her* whole. This is not an isolated inference from one narrative detail: the same pattern is taught in Jesus's touching the leper (Mark 1:40-42, another ritually unclean condition met with contact rather than avoidance) and in his table fellowship with tax collectors and sinners (Mark 2:15-17, the same principle in a different register — he is not compromised by proximity to what is unclean or sinful; he cleanses and restores it).

## Application

- Where the text draws a line worth respecting: the specific mechanics of Levitical ritual purity — what transmits uncleanness, for how long, what washing is required — were ceremonial law for Israel under the Old Covenant, and are not binding requirements on believers now (see AGENTS.md's ceremonial/civil/moral distinction for law texts generally).
- What transcends the original setting: the underlying pattern — that a person's felt disqualification (whether from sin, shame, social status, or physical condition) does not block access to Christ, and that he is not diminished or defiled by contact with what the world calls unclean — is taught repeatedly across the Gospels, not just implied here.
- A question worth sitting with rather than answering too quickly: the woman approached covertly, expecting (reasonably, under the law as she understood it) that open contact would be refused or rebuked. Jesus's response is not correction for the covert approach, but public acknowledgment ("he looked around to see who had done this," 5:32) that draws her out of hiding and names her "daughter." What does it mean that faith doesn't need to already understand itself correctly, or approach with full confidence, to be met this way?

## Discussion questions

1. What does the reversal of Leviticus 15:27's contact-transmits-uncleanness logic in this passage suggest about how Jesus relates to what Torah calls "unclean" more broadly?
2. Why might Mark have chosen to interrupt the Jairus story rather than simply tell the two healings back to back? What would be lost if they were separated?
3. Mark 10:52 uses the identical phrase "your faith has healed/saved you" for Bartimaeus. Read both passages together — what do the two accounts share, and where do they differ?
4. The text never uses explicit purity-law vocabulary ("unclean," "impure") — it simply narrates the facts (a twelve-year discharge, a covert touch from behind) and expects the reader to supply the Levitical background. What does that assume about Mark's original audience, and what does a modern reader need to know that they didn't?
